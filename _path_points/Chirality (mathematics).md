<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, a figure is <b>chiral</b> (and said to have <b>chirality</b>) if it is not identical to its <a href="/wiki/Mirror_image" title="Mirror image">mirror image</a>, or, more precisely, if it cannot be mapped to its mirror image by <a href="/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics)" title="Rotation (mathematics)">rotations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Translation_(geometry)" title="Translation (geometry)">translations</a> alone. An object that is not chiral is said to be achiral. In 3 dimensions, not all achiral objects have a mirror plane. For example, a 3-dimensional object with <a href="/wiki/Inversion_centre" title="Inversion centre" class="mw-redirect">inversion centre</a> as its only nontrivial <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_operation" title="Symmetry operation">symmetry operation</a> is achiral but has no mirror plane.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A chiral object and its mirror image are said to be <b>enantiomorphs</b>. The word <i>chirality</i> is derived from the Greek <span lang="grc">χείρ</span> (cheir), the hand, the most familiar chiral object; the word <i>enantiomorph</i> stems from the Greek <span lang="grc">ἐναντίος</span> (enantios) 'opposite' + <span lang="grc">μορφή</span> (morphe) 'form'.  A non-chiral figure is called <b>achiral</b> or <b>amphichiral</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chirality_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Some chiral three-dimensional objects, such as the <a href="/wiki/Helix" title="Helix">helix</a>, can be assigned a right or left <a href="/wiki/Handedness" title="Handedness">handedness</a>, according to the <a href="/wiki/Right-hand_rule" title="Right-hand rule">right-hand rule</a>.
</p><p>Many other familiar objects exhibit the same chiral symmetry of the human body, such as gloves and shoes. A right shoe is different from a left shoe only for being mirror images of each other. In contrast thin gloves may not be considered chiral if you can wear them <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inside_out" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:inside out">inside-out</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The J, L, S and Z-shaped <i><a href="/wiki/Tetromino" title="Tetromino">tetrominoes</a></i> of the popular video game <a href="/wiki/Tetris" title="Tetris">Tetris</a> also exhibit chirality, but only in a two-dimensional space. Individually they contain no mirror symmetry in the plane.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chirality_and_symmetry_group">Chirality and symmetry group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chirality_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chirality and symmetry group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A figure is achiral if and only if its <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a> contains at least one <i>orientation-reversing</i> isometry. (In Euclidean geometry any <a href="/wiki/Isometry" title="Isometry">isometry</a> can be written as <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="v\mapsto Av+b" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/8/3/783ea4533900cf3c14f45b16ad9687ba.png" /> with an <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix" title="Orthogonal matrix">orthogonal matrix</a> <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="A" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/f/c/7fc56270e7a70fa81a5935b72eacbe29.png" /> and a vector <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="b" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/2/e/92eb5ffee6ae2fec3ad71c777531578f.png" />. The <a href="/wiki/Determinant" title="Determinant">determinant</a> of <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="A" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/f/c/7fc56270e7a70fa81a5935b72eacbe29.png" /> is either 1 or &#8722;1 then. If it is &#8722;1 the isometry is <i><a href="/wiki/Orientation_(mathematics)" title="Orientation (mathematics)" class="mw-redirect">orientation</a>-reversing</i>, otherwise it is orientation-preserving.)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chirality_in_three_dimensions">Chirality in three dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chirality_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chirality in three dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chiralit%C3%A4t_von_W%C3%BCrfeln_V.1.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Chiralit%C3%A4t_von_W%C3%BCrfeln_V.1.svg/220px-Chiralit%C3%A4t_von_W%C3%BCrfeln_V.1.svg.png" width="220" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Chiralit%C3%A4t_von_W%C3%BCrfeln_V.1.svg/330px-Chiralit%C3%A4t_von_W%C3%BCrfeln_V.1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Chiralit%C3%A4t_von_W%C3%BCrfeln_V.1.svg/440px-Chiralit%C3%A4t_von_W%C3%BCrfeln_V.1.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="825" data-file-height="966" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chiralit%C3%A4t_von_W%C3%BCrfeln_V.1.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pair of chiral <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a> (enantiomorphs)</div></div></div>
<p>In three dimensions, every figure that possesses a <a href="/wiki/Mirror_plane_of_symmetry" title="Mirror plane of symmetry" class="mw-redirect">mirror plane of symmetry</a> <i>S<sub>1</sub></i>, an inversion center of symmetry <i>S<sub>2</sub></i>, or a higher <a href="/wiki/Improper_rotation" title="Improper rotation">improper rotation</a> (rotoreflection) <i>S<sub>n</sub></i> axis of symmetry <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> is achiral. (A <i>plane of symmetry</i> of a figure <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="F" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/0/0/800618943025315f869e4e1f09471012.png" /> is a plane <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="P" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/4/c/44c29edb103a2872f519ad0c9a0fdaaa.png" />, such that <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="F" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/0/0/800618943025315f869e4e1f09471012.png" /> is invariant under the mapping <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="(x,y,z)\mapsto(x,y,-z)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/8/d/f8dcb1cc15e72123df79e47e1ad0032d.png" />, when <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="P" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/4/c/44c29edb103a2872f519ad0c9a0fdaaa.png" /> is chosen to be the <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="x" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png" />-<img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="y" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/5/415290769594460e2e485922904f345d.png" />-plane of the coordinate system. A <i>center of symmetry</i> of a figure <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="F" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/0/0/800618943025315f869e4e1f09471012.png" /> is a point <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="C" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/d/6/0d61f8370cad1d412f80b84d143e1257.png" />, such that <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="F" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/0/0/800618943025315f869e4e1f09471012.png" /> is invariant under the mapping <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="(x,y,z)\mapsto(-x,-y,-z)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/6/2/1620fd53d6f66d0fabf51c67d9f74dbf.png" />, when <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="C" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/d/6/0d61f8370cad1d412f80b84d143e1257.png" /> is chosen to be the origin of the coordinate system.) Note, however, that there are achiral figures lacking both plane and center of symmetry. An example is the figure
</p>
<dl><dd><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="F_0=\left\{(1,0,0),(0,1,0),(-1,0,0),(0,-1,0),(2,1,1),(-1,2,-1),(-2,-1,1),(1,-2,-1)\right\}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/5/0/b5064b2a4b20a23d1011de02ac8eaa30.png" /></dd></dl>
<p>which is invariant under the orientation reversing isometry <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="(x,y,z)\mapsto(-y,x,-z)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/6/6/b6623257e9e1397fe5d119f7bf1b1136.png" /> and thus achiral, but it has neither plane nor center of symmetry. The figure
</p>
<dl><dd><img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="F_1=\left\{(1,0,0),(-1,0,0),(0,2,0),(0,-2,0),(1,1,1),(-1,-1,-1)\right\}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/8/4/b848fe17a988191ba33f7a1febb5170a.png" /></dd></dl>
<p>also is achiral as the origin is a center of symmetry, but it lacks a plane of symmetry.
</p><p>Note also that achiral figures can have a <a href="/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions#Center_of_symmetry" title="Point groups in three dimensions">center axis</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chirality_in_two_dimensions">Chirality in two dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chirality_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chirality in two dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bracelets33.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bracelets33.svg/300px-Bracelets33.svg.png" width="300" height="94" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bracelets33.svg/450px-Bracelets33.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Bracelets33.svg/600px-Bracelets33.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1545" data-file-height="482" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bracelets33.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The colored <a href="/wiki/Necklace_(combinatorics)" title="Necklace (combinatorics)">bracelet</a> in the middle is <b>chiral</b> in 2-dimensions, the two others are <b>achiral</b>. The left and right images contain a horizontal reflection line.</div></div></div>
<p>In two dimensions, every figure which possesses an <a href="/wiki/Axis_of_symmetry" title="Axis of symmetry" class="mw-redirect">axis of symmetry</a> is achiral, and it can be shown that every <i>bounded</i> achiral figure must have an axis of symmetry. (An <i>axis of symmetry</i> of a figure <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="F" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/0/0/800618943025315f869e4e1f09471012.png" /> is a line <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/2/0/d20caec3b48a1eef164cb4ca81ba2587.png" />, such that <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="F" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/0/0/800618943025315f869e4e1f09471012.png" /> is invariant under the mapping <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="(x,y)\mapsto(x,-y)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/2/b/82b297f66f5cf6d4059a9f4aff43b500.png" />, when <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="L" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/2/0/d20caec3b48a1eef164cb4ca81ba2587.png" /> is chosen to be the <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="x" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png" />-axis of the coordinate system.) Consider the following pattern:
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/File:Krok_6.png" class="image"><img alt="Krok 6.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Krok_6.png/320px-Krok_6.png" width="320" height="54" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Krok_6.png/480px-Krok_6.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Krok_6.png/640px-Krok_6.png 2x" data-file-width="1190" data-file-height="202" /></a></dd></dl>
<p>This figure is chiral, as it is not identical to its mirror image:
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/File:Krok_6_mirrored.png" class="image"><img alt="Krok 6 mirrored.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Krok_6_mirrored.png/320px-Krok_6_mirrored.png" width="320" height="54" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Krok_6_mirrored.png/480px-Krok_6_mirrored.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Krok_6_mirrored.png/640px-Krok_6_mirrored.png 2x" data-file-width="1190" data-file-height="202" /></a></dd></dl>
<p>But if one prolongs the pattern in both directions to infinity, one receives an (unbounded) achiral figure which has no axis of symmetry. Its symmetry group is a <a href="/wiki/Frieze_group" title="Frieze group">frieze group</a> generated by a single <a href="/wiki/Glide_reflection" title="Glide reflection">glide reflection</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Knot_theory">Knot theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chirality_(mathematics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Knot theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)" title="Knot (mathematics)">knot</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Amphichiral_knot" title="Amphichiral knot" class="mw-redirect">achiral</a> if it can be continuously deformed into its mirror image, otherwise it is called a <a href="/wiki/Chiral_knot" title="Chiral knot">chiral knot</a>. For example the <a href="/wiki/Unknot" title="Unknot">unknot</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Figure-eight_knot_(mathematics)" title="Figure-eight knot (mathematics)">figure-eight knot</a> are achiral, whereas the <a href="/wiki/Trefoil_knot" title="Trefoil knot">trefoil knot</a> is chiral.
</p>
