<p><a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">Mathematical</a> ideas have been used as inspiration for a number of <a href="/wiki/Fiber_art" title="Fiber art">fiber arts</a> including <a href="/wiki/Quilt" title="Quilt">quilt</a> <a href="/wiki/Quilting" title="Quilting">making</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knitting" title="Knitting">knitting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross-stitch" title="Cross-stitch">cross-stitch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crochet" title="Crochet">crochet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embroidery" title="Embroidery">embroidery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a>. A wide range of mathematical concepts have been used as inspiration including <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graph theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a>. Some techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Counted-thread_embroidery" title="Counted-thread embroidery">counted-thread embroidery</a> are naturally <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometrical</a>; other kinds of <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> provide a ready means for the colorful <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_and_art" title="Mathematics and art">physical expression of mathematical concepts</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quilting">Quilting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathematics_and_fiber_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Quilting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IEEE_Spectrum" title="IEEE Spectrum">IEEE Spectrum</a> has organized a number of competitions on <a href="/wiki/Quilt_block" title="Quilt block" class="mw-redirect">quilt block</a> design, and several books have been published on the subject. Notable quiltmakers include Diana Venters and Elaine Ellison, who have written a book on the subject <i>Mathematical Quilts: No Sewing Required</i>. Examples of mathematical ideas used in the book as the basis of a quilt include the <a href="/wiki/Golden_rectangle" title="Golden rectangle">golden rectangle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conic_section" title="Conic section">conic sections</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>'s Claw, the <a href="/wiki/Koch_curve" title="Koch curve" class="mw-redirect">Koch curve</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Clifford_torus" title="Clifford torus">Clifford torus</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Gaku" title="San Gaku" class="mw-redirect">San Gaku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Mascheroni" title="Lorenzo Mascheroni">Mascheroni</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cardioid" title="Cardioid">cardioid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_triple" title="Pythagorean triple">Pythagorean triples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spidron" title="Spidron">spidrons</a>, and the six <a href="/wiki/Trigonometric_functions" title="Trigonometric functions">trigonometric functions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Knitting_and_crochet">Knitting and crochet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathematics_and_fiber_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Knitting and crochet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Knitted mathematical objects include the <a href="/wiki/Platonic_solid" title="Platonic solid">Platonic solids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Klein_bottle" title="Klein bottle">Klein bottles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boy%27s_surface" title="Boy&#39;s surface">Boy's surface</a>.
The <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_attractor" title="Lorenz attractor" class="mw-redirect">Lorenz manifold</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_manifold" title="Hyperbolic manifold">hyperbolic plane</a> have been crafted using crochet.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Knitted and crocheted <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">tori</a> have also been constructed depicting <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_graph" title="Toroidal graph">toroidal embeddings</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Complete_graph" title="Complete graph">complete graph</a> <i>K</i><sub>7</sub> and of the <a href="/wiki/Heawood_graph" title="Heawood graph">Heawood graph</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The crocheting of hyperbolic planes has been popularized by the <a href="/wiki/Institute_For_Figuring" title="Institute For Figuring">Institute For Figuring</a>; a book by <a href="/wiki/Daina_Taimina" title="Daina Taimina">Daina Taimina</a> on the subject, <i>Crocheting Adventures with Hyperbolic Planes</i>, won the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Bookseller/Diagram_Prize_for_Oddest_Title_of_the_Year" title="Bookseller/Diagram Prize for Oddest Title of the Year">Bookseller/Diagram Prize for Oddest Title of the Year</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p>

<p>Embroidery techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Counted-thread_embroidery" title="Counted-thread embroidery">counted-thread embroidery</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Cross-stitch" title="Cross-stitch">cross-stitch</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Canvas_work" title="Canvas work">canvas work</a> methods such as <a href="/wiki/Bargello_(needlework)" title="Bargello (needlework)">Bargello (needlework)</a> make use of the natural <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a> of the weave, lending themselves to geometric designs.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weaving">Weaving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathematics_and_fiber_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Weaving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ada_Dietz" title="Ada Dietz">Ada Dietz</a> (1882 &#8211; 1950) was an American <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaver</a> best known for her 1949 monograph <i>Algebraic Expressions in Handwoven Textiles</i>, which defines weaving patterns based on the expansion of multivariate <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">polynomials</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/J._C._P._Miller" title="J. C. P. Miller">J. C. P.&#32;Miller</a>&#160;(<a href="#CITEREFMiller1970">1970</a>) used the <a href="/wiki/Rule_90" title="Rule 90">Rule 90</a> <a href="/wiki/Cellular_automaton" title="Cellular automaton">cellular automaton</a> to design <a href="/wiki/Tapestry" title="Tapestry">tapestries</a> depicting both trees and abstract patterns of triangles.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spinning">Spinning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathematics_and_fiber_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spinning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Margaret_Greig" title="Margaret Greig">Margaret Greig</a> was a mathematician who articulated the mathematics of <a href="/wiki/Worsted_spinning" title="Worsted spinning" class="mw-redirect">worsted spinning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fashion_design">Fashion design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathematics_and_fiber_arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fashion design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The silk scarves from DMCK Designs' 2013 collection<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> are all based on <a href="/wiki/Space-filling_curve" title="Space-filling curve">space-filling curve</a> patterns that Doug McKenna has devised or discovered.  The designs are either generalized Peano curves, or based on a new space-filling construction technique.  Both techniques are described in short papers available in the Bridges Math and Art conference proceedings (2007 and 2008).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Issey_Miyake" title="Issey Miyake">Issey Miyake</a> Fall-Winter 2010–2011 ready-to-wear collection featured designs from a collaboration between fashion designer Dai Fujiwara and mathematician <a href="/wiki/William_Thurston" title="William Thurston">William Thurston</a>. The designs were inspired by Thurston's <a href="/wiki/Geometrization_conjecture" title="Geometrization conjecture">geometrization conjecture</a>, the statement that every <a href="/wiki/3-manifold" title="3-manifold">3-manifold</a> can be decomposed into pieces with one of eight different uniform geometries, a proof of which had been sketched in 2003 by <a href="/wiki/Grigori_Perelman" title="Grigori Perelman">Grigori Perelman</a> as part of his proof of the <a href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture" title="Poincaré conjecture">Poincaré conjecture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p>
