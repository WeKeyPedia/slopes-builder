<p>The most common <b>relative <a href="/wiki/Direction_(geometry)" title="Direction (geometry)">directions</a></b> are <b><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/left" class="extiw" title="wikt:left">left</a></b>, <b><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/right" class="extiw" title="wikt:right">right</a></b>, <b><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/forward" class="extiw" title="wikt:forward">forward(s)</a></b>, <b><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/backward" class="extiw" title="wikt:backward">backward(s)</a></b>, <b><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/up" class="extiw" title="wikt:up">up</a></b>, and <b><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/down" class="extiw" title="wikt:down">down</a></b>. No <a href="/wiki/Absolute_value" title="Absolute value">absolute</a> direction corresponds to any of the relative directions. This is a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Translational_symmetry" title="Translational symmetry">translational invariance</a> of the laws of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>: nature, loosely speaking, behaves the same no matter what direction one moves. As demonstrated by the <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="Michelson–Morley experiment">Michelson-Morley null result</a>, there is no absolute <a href="/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference" title="Inertial frame of reference">inertial frame of reference</a>. There are definite relationships between the relative directions, however. Left and right, forward and backward, and up and down are three pairs of <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/complementary" class="extiw" title="wikt:complementary">complementary</a> directions, each pair <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal" title="Orthogonal" class="mw-redirect">orthogonal</a> to both of the others. Relative directions are also known as <b>egocentric coordinates</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions_and_conventions">Traditions and conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relative_direction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Traditions and conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since definitions of left and right based on the geometry of the natural environment are unwieldy, in practice, the meaning of relative direction words is conveyed through <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">tradition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acculturation" title="Acculturation">acculturation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, and direct reference. One common definition of up and down uses <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity" class="mw-redirect">gravity</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">planet Earth</a> as a frame of reference. Since there is a very noticeable force of gravity acting between the Earth and any other nearby object, down is defined as that direction which an object moves in reference to the Earth when the object is allowed to <a href="/wiki/Free-fall" title="Free-fall" class="mw-redirect">fall freely</a>. Up is then defined as the opposite direction of down.  Another common definition uses a human body, standing upright, as a frame of reference. In that case, up is defined as the direction from feet to head, perpendicular to the surface of the Earth. In most cases, up is a directionally oriented position generally opposite to that of the pull of gravity.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orestes_(1848).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Orestes_%281848%29.jpg/170px-Orestes_%281848%29.jpg" width="170" height="257" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Orestes_%281848%29.jpg/255px-Orestes_%281848%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Orestes_%281848%29.jpg/340px-Orestes_%281848%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="676" data-file-height="1020" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orestes_(1848).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This statue holds a sword in its <a href="/wiki/Proper_right" title="Proper right">proper right</a> hand</div></div></div>
<p>In situations where a common frame of reference is needed, it is most common to use an egocentric view. A simple example is road signage. Another example is <a href="/wiki/Blocking_(stage)" title="Blocking (stage)">stage blocking</a>, where "stage left" "stage right" "stage up" and "stage down" are, by convention, defined from the actor's point of view, but up and down stage do not follow gravitational conventions of up and down. An example of a non-egocentric view is <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">page layout</a>, where the relative terms "upper half" "left margin," etc. are defined in terms of the observer but employed in reverse for a <a href="/wiki/Compositor" title="Compositor">type compositor</a>, returning to an egocentric view. In medicine and science, where precise definitions are crucial, relative directions (left and right) are the sides of the organism, not those of the observer. The same is true in heraldry, where left and right in a <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> is treated as if the shield was being held by the armiger. To avoid confusion, Latin terminology is employed: <i>dexter</i> and <i>sinister</i> for right and left.  <a href="/wiki/Proper_right" title="Proper right">Proper right and proper left</a> are terms mainly used to describe images, and overcome the potential confusion that a figure's right or "proper right" hand is on the left hand as the viewer of a frontal image sees it.
</p><p>Forward and backward may be defined by referring to an object's or person's <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">motion</a>. Forward is defined as the direction in which the object is moving. Backward is then defined as the opposite direction to forward. Alternatively, 'forward' may be the direction pointed by the observer's <a href="/wiki/Human_nose" title="Human nose">nose</a>, defining 'backward' as the direction from the nose to the <a href="/wiki/Parietal_bone" title="Parietal bone">sagittal border</a> in the observer's <a href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">skull</a>. With respect to a ship 'forward' would indicate the relative position of any object lying in the direction the ship is pointing. For symmetrical objects, it is also necessary to define forward and backward in terms of expected direction. Many mass transit trains are built symmetrically with paired control booths, and definitions of forward, backward, left, and right are temporary.
</p><p>Given significant distance from the <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_pole" title="Geomagnetic pole">magnetic poles</a>, one can figure which hand is which using a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_compass" title="Magnetic compass" class="mw-redirect">magnetic compass</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>. Facing the sun, before noon, the north pointer of the compass points to the "left" hand. After noon, it points to the "right".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geometry_of_the_natural_environment">Geometry of the natural environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relative_direction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geometry of the natural environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sorts_on_composing_stick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sorts_on_composing_stick.jpg/220px-Sorts_on_composing_stick.jpg" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sorts_on_composing_stick.jpg/330px-Sorts_on_composing_stick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sorts_on_composing_stick.jpg/440px-Sorts_on_composing_stick.jpg 2x" data-file-width="638" data-file-height="517" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sorts_on_composing_stick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Type compositing</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Right-hand_rule" title="Right-hand rule">right-hand rule</a> is one common way to relate the three principal directions. For many years a <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">fundamental question</a> in physics was whether a left-hand rule would be equivalent. Many <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">natural structures</a>, including human bodies, follow a certain "<a href="/wiki/Handedness" title="Handedness">handedness</a>", but it was widely assumed that nature did not distinguish the two possibilities. This changed with the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)#Parity_violation" title="Parity (physics)">parity violations</a> in <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>. If a sample of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt-60</a> <a href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms" class="mw-redirect">atoms</a> is <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetized</a> so that they spin <a href="/wiki/Counterclockwise" title="Counterclockwise" class="mw-redirect">counterclockwise</a> around some <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">axis</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Beta_radiation" title="Beta radiation" class="mw-redirect">beta radiation</a> resulting from their <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_decay" title="Nuclear decay" class="mw-redirect">nuclear decay</a> will be preferentially directed opposite that axis. Since counter-clockwise may be defined in terms of up, forward, and right, this experiment unambiguously differentiates left from right using only natural elements: if they were reversed, or the atoms spun <a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">clockwise</a>, the radiation would follow the spin axis instead of being opposite to it.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nautical_terminology">Nautical terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relative_direction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nautical terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bow_(ship)" title="Bow (ship)">Bow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_and_starboard" title="Port and starboard">port, and starboard</a> are nautical terms that convey an impersonal relative direction in the context of the moving frame of persons aboard a ship. The need for impersonal terms is most clearly seen in a rowing shell where the majority of the crew face aft ("backwards") and the oars to their right are actually on the port side.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultures_not_using_relative_directions">Cultures not using relative directions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relative_direction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultures not using relative directions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most human cultures use relative directions for reference, but there are exceptions. The Australian Aboriginal people the <a href="/wiki/Guugu_Yimithirr_people" title="Guugu Yimithirr people" class="mw-redirect">Guugu Yimithirr</a> have no words denoting the egocentric directions in their <a href="/wiki/Guugu_Yimithirr_language" title="Guugu Yimithirr language">language</a>; instead, they exclusively refer to <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_directions" title="Cardinal directions" class="mw-redirect">cardinal directions</a>, even when describing small-scale spaces. For instance, if they wanted someone to move over on the car seat to make room, they might say "move a bit to the east". To tell someone where exactly they left something in their house, they might say, "I left it on the southern edge of the western table." Or they might warn a person to "look out for that big ant just north of your foot". Other peoples "from Polynesia to Mexico and from Namibia to Bali" similarly have predominantly "geographic languages".<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/American_Sign_Language" title="American Sign Language">American Sign Language</a> makes heavy use of geographical direction through absolute orientation.<span class="cleanup-needed-content" style="background-color: #fff9f9; color: DarkSlateGray; border: 1px solid #ffdcdc;"></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> When speaking <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a>, speakers will typically use the words for "front", "back", "left", and "right" to mean "south", "north", "east", and "west", respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Left-right_confusion"><span id="Left.2Fright_confusion"></span>Left-right confusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Relative_direction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Left-right confusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Left-right confusion</b> is the difficulty some people have in distinguishing the difference between the directions left and right. According to research by John R. Clarke (<a href="/wiki/Drexel_University" title="Drexel University">Drexel University</a>) it affects about 15% of the population.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These people can usually normally perform daily activities such as driving according to signs and navigating according to a map, but will often take a wrong turn when told to turn left or right and may have difficulties performing actions that require precise understanding of directional commands, such as <a href="/wiki/Ballroom_dancing" title="Ballroom dancing" class="mw-redirect">ballroom dancing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brandt-Mackavey_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brandt-Mackavey-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hannay_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hannay-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup>
</p>
